WIL: A Reflection

Over 12 weeks, I worked under Dr Yong Wu in Institute for Integrated and Intelligent Systems (IIIS) to construct an optimization approach to black-box functions. A lot of research and trial-and-error was needed in this complex work-integrated-learning (WIL) project. Here I reflect on this valuable experience.

Being an Expert: To be, or not to be

My industry supervisor briefed me on the nature of the project and provided me with multiple technical papers to read. Initially, I felt a clear sense of guidance and direction as the task of reading papers seemed simple. I encountered many unknown terms and technical jargon once I started reading these papers. I felt increasingly disoriented and compelled to understand everything, even though the context and application were different to my placement. I became fixated with learning the applications of a certain algorithm and spent hours listening to YouTube tutorials and reading technical papers. I felt less confident about my capability, and that I was not ready to translate literature into code.

I believed the solution to my doubts was to obtain a depth of knowledge in every algorithm I encountered. I now understand that focusing on depth in one area (e.g., algorithm function) led to a considerable waste of time as being an expert on one of the many learning points meant less time could be allocated to learning other relevant information/skills. I will be needed to constantly learn new software, languages and domain knowledge in my career in IT. Hence to tackle this issue in the future, I shall compile a list of questions to guide my research and learning process and set the scope of depth and breadth for relevant topics/skills. I will utilise a note-taking application such as Evernote to consolidate my knowledge. This will help me focus, and not get sidetracked into learning more about a term/domain/skill than needed to carry out tasks. I am aware that in areas where I need to be an expert, delving deep into one domain will allow me to discern the many applications of the knowledge area.

With deadlines approaching, I proceeded to try out snippets of code that I did not fully understand to mimic Blackbox functions. I was surprised when I made progress with building a model, and this challenged my assumption that I cannot apply with having a beginner level of knowledge/skills. My small achievement taught me that I should not doubt my capabilities and be hesitant to try just because I do not have expertise. Additionally, I understood the importance of segmenting tasks with proper time allocation and discipline such that all learning tasks are completed. I realised that not being an ‚Äúexpert‚Äù on things I learn does not make me less capable. It is important to recognise when a breadth of knowledge/skills may be more beneficial than having expertise.

Receiving Feedback and communication methods

After reading numerous publications, I explored two algorithms in-depth without knowing if I was heading in the right direction. Although I wanted to be error-free in my work, I was uneasy about receiving negative feedback that I misunderstood the tasks assigned or my methods were wrong. Instead of approaching the placement supervisor after making small progress (e.g., learning about Blackbox optimization algorithms), I opted to do as much work as possible independently for many weeks. I realise that I desired to prove my capability to not just the supervisor, but myself. It was frustrating when I self-discovered that I was heading in the wrong direction as it was time-consuming and needed numerous trials and errors. I eventually approached the supervisor for guidance and validation when I could not progress further. After talking to the supervisor, I now understand that receiving feedback is not necessarily daunting as it is not harsh criticism. Furthermore, it does not undermine my capabilities or make my existing work futile, as sometimes we must experience failures to know what is correct. I recognise that I pressurised myself to be perfect, but this was not expected of me since I am new to many things.

I now understand that there is a distinction between overreliance and communicating to receive feedback. Constantly wanting to verify without self-exploration may come off as dependence, while periodically checking (e.g., once a week or fortnightly) is necessary. In all prospective jobs, I will work on my communication skills and practice asking for feedback and guidance whenever needed. Additionally, I shall learn to graciously accept and utilise both positive and negative feedback without getting defensive. While this skill cannot be acquired overnight, I will read on tips and mindfully practice it in my future workplace/s.

I communicated with my placement supervisor through emails, physical meetings, and video calls. I realised that physical meetings make me feel intimidated, and it is harder to ask questions and process feedback. However, I also recognised that for different purposes, different communication methods work best (e.g., for many questions, talking is better than emailing). I am aware that in my career, I will need to be open to all methods of communication and thus, I need to overcome my discomfort with face-to-face meetings.

Technical Skills

A reflection of the technical & transferable skills I gained and polished through my WIL placement.

Understanding Algorithms & their Uses

My placement supervisor gave me the task of devising an optimisation algorithm for Blackbox functions. He assigned me the task of reading multiple papers on the uses of Blackbox functions in various domains (e.g., mining). Before this placement, I had never heard of Blackbox functions or derivative free optimization algorithms. I knew some commonly used gradient based optimization algorithms (Regression, Gradient descent) and that algorithms can be easily implemented with libraries (e.g., Scikit-learn for Python).

To understand how Blackbox optimization works, I read the assigned readings to understand how it functions and its uses. However, the papers had many unfamiliar technical jargons, and it prompted further research. I watched numerous YouTube tutorials because they had more visualisations and simpler explanations than technical papers. After gaining a primary understanding of Blackbox optimization, I re-read technical papers as the technical jargon was more palatable. After spending considerable time learning about Blackbox functions, I then proceeded to explore open-sourced algorithm codes (e.g., on GitHub) that aim to optimise them. To build the optimization algorithm, I read existing optimization implementations on GitHub and formulated basic Blackbox optimization models from that knowledge. I should have spent more time exploring and testing algorithms that optimise these functions as the aim of placement was to create algorithms with state-of-the-art solutions/accuracy. However, now I have gained an intermediate level of understanding of Blackbox functions which will allow me to apply this knowledge in many domains (e.g., finance, science, engineering). This is not common knowledge in IT and thus, it will distinguish me from others. Furthermore, the research and building process improved my understanding of algorithms as I looked at the classes, functions, and parameters that define the algorithm (depth). It also gave me exposure to more algorithms and increased my breadth of algorithm knowledge. As a Data Analyst, I will be required to identify the most appropriate algorithm to implement on datasets. This experience will allow me to make better data-related choices and transfer optimization skills to other domains of data analytics such as Hyperparameter optimization (choosing the best parameters for a machine learning model).

I struggled to construct an optimization algorithm from scratch as it is a complex and time-consuming process. I learnt that in developing algorithms, I cannot rely entirely on existing algorithm codes as they are either too generic or tailored to specific purposes. I must first come up with the logic by being clear about its uses and then translate it to codes. I learnt that I need to improve my mathematical knowledge, especially Linear Algebra, which will allow me to understand how algorithms work and subsequently help me create and apply them better.
